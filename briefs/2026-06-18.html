Smart in 30 Seconds

Fed Chair Kevin Warsh made his policy debut Wednesday and delivered a hawkish jolt: the Fed held rates at 3.50%–3.75% but stripped its easing bias from the statement, and 9 of 18 FOMC officials now project at least one rate hike before year-end — sending the S&P 500 down 1.2% and 10-year yields up 7 basis points on Wednesday in what Schwab called the worst Fed-day performance for a new chair since 1994. Markets attempted a partial recovery Thursday, lifted by a major catalyst: President Trump announced via Truth Social that Apple has agreed to work with Intel to design and build chips in the United States, sending Intel shares surging more than 10% in pre-market. Simultaneously, the US-Iran peace memorandum of understanding — signed Wednesday evening at the Palace of Versailles — took formal effect, accelerating oil's decline with WTI falling to roughly $75.83/barrel as the prospect of Strait of Hormuz reopening added supply expectations to an already-softening market. Thursday is the last trading session before a Juneteenth long weekend, with triple-witching Friday closed, leaving investors to digest a week packed with geopolitical, monetary policy, and industrial-policy catalysts.

01 — What Moved Markets Today

Three Seismic Signals in 24 Hours: Warsh, Hormuz, and the Intel Revival

Monetary Policy — The Warsh Hawkish Shock

The Federal Reserve's June FOMC meeting, the first chaired by Kevin Warsh, concluded Wednesday with the committee unanimously voting to hold the federal funds rate steady at 3.50%–3.75% — the fourth consecutive hold. But the headline rate decision was far from the real story. The updated Summary of Economic Projections (dot plot) showed the median year-end 2026 rate at 3.8%, revised up sharply from 3.4% in the March projections. Nine of the 18 officials who submitted projections indicated rates should finish 2026 above the current range — effectively signaling a hike is on the table — while Warsh himself declined to submit a dot, breaking with tradition and citing philosophical opposition to forward guidance as a policy tool.

The post-meeting statement was pared to just 130 words, stripping all easing-bias language and closing with a blunt declaration: the FOMC "will deliver price stability." Investors who had positioned for a dovish Warsh debut were caught wrong-footed. The S&P 500 shed 1.2% on the day — its worst Fed-day performance for a new chair's first meeting since 1994 according to Schwab. The 2-year Treasury yield soared 14 basis points to 4.19%, while the 10-year yield rose 7 basis points to 4.49%. All 11 S&P 500 sectors closed in the red Wednesday.

Industrial Policy — Trump Announces Intel-Apple Domestic Chip Deal

In the early hours of Thursday, President Trump posted on Truth Social that Apple had agreed to work with Intel to design and build its chips in the United States. The announcement triggered a premarket surge of more than 9% in Intel shares, which had closed at $121.10 on Wednesday. Intel's stock has already surged more than 228% year-to-date, supported by strong Q1 results where revenue rose 7% to $13.6 billion, beating estimates by about 9%, and the Data Center and AI segment posted 22% growth.

The deal — first reported in preliminary form by the Wall Street Journal in May — would see Apple, which currently relies heavily on TSMC for its advanced A-series and M-series chip manufacturing, diversify its foundry relationships to include Intel's 18A-P process node. Intel said Wednesday that the 18A-P process had entered risk production, delivering up to 9% higher performance and 18% lower power consumption versus the standard 18A. Neither Apple nor Intel issued a formal statement confirming the deal, and analysts noted the stock move was pricing in revenue that could be 12–18 months away.

Geopolitics — US-Iran MOU Signed; Strait of Hormuz Set to Reopen

President Trump and Iranian President Masoud Pezeshkian signed a 14-point memorandum of understanding Wednesday evening at the Palace of Versailles, formally initiating an end to the US-Israel conflict with Iran that began February 28. The MOU declares the immediate and permanent termination of military operations, commits both sides to further talks toward a final deal within 60 days, and — critically for energy markets — obligates Iran to arrange safe passage of commercial vessels through the Strait of Hormuz. Trump simultaneously ordered the removal of the U.S. naval blockade of Iranian ports.

The implications for oil were immediate and significant. WTI crude fell to roughly $75.83 per barrel on Thursday, extending a decline of around 38% from the conflict-peak highs reached in April. The IEA has warned that the Strait of Hormuz reopening could push the global oil market into a significant supply surplus by 2027, with global supply projected to rise by 8 million barrels per day against demand growth of just 2 million bpd. Goldman Sachs noted Gulf exports are now expected to normalize by end of July, though flows may initially recover to only 70% of pre-war levels.

Macro Data — PPI Points to Firm PCE Ahead

This morning's Producer Price Index data added another layer of complexity. Components of the May PPI that feed into the Fed's preferred inflation gauge — the PCE price index — suggest a firm upcoming PCE print. Only the air transport component declined. This matters because May PCE data is due at the end of the month and will serve as a critical input to the September FOMC meeting, which RSM has described as a "live" policy meeting where the committee could see movement. The 10-year yield, at 4.49%, is trading consistent with this cautious inflation outlook, with the curve positively sloped: the 2-year at 4.20%, the 10-year at 4.49%, and the 30-year at 4.93%.

02 — Why It Mattered

The End of Dovish Assumptions — and the Start of a More Uncertain Market

Wednesday's Fed meeting was not just a policy hold. It was a communication overhaul with market-moving consequences. For more than a year, investors had priced in a Fed that was gradually moving toward easier policy — the so-called "easing bias" embedded in every FOMC statement. Warsh ripped that language out in his first act as chair. By stripping forward guidance, he simultaneously removed a key anchor for equity valuations and raised the probability distribution of outcomes for rates. Markets don't like uncertainty; they discount it, and that's exactly what happened Wednesday.

The hawkish dot-plot shift is particularly significant because inflation has not cooperated with the Fed's 2% target. Core inflation has been hovering around 4.2% year-over-year — well above the current fed funds rate of 3.50–3.75% and above virtually every inflation metric the Fed tracks, according to RSM. That means real rates are still negative on some measures, and a committee that is genuinely committed to price stability — as Warsh's terse 130-word statement implied — has reason to act if inflation doesn't cool. The September meeting is now, per RSM, "a live policy meeting where the committee could see movement."

The Intel-Apple story operates on a different but equally important frequency: domestic semiconductor supply chains. Trump has used Intel as a cornerstone of his industrial policy narrative, and if the Apple relationship formalizes, it would be a landmark commercial validation for Intel Foundry — a business that has been losing billions as it attempts to compete with TSMC. Intel Foundry's operating loss narrowed to $2.4 billion last quarter, improving by $72 million quarter-over-quarter. The Q2 earnings report on July 23 will be the first real checkpoint on whether foundry customer commitments are translating from conversations to contracts.

The Iran MOU carries profound second-order effects beyond oil prices. The Strait of Hormuz had been effectively closed since February 28, blocking an estimated 14 million barrels per day of Middle Eastern oil output according to the IEA. Nearly 600 ships and 20,000 seafarers were stranded in Gulf waters. The reopening — even partially and cautiously — represents a massive supply-side unlock that changes the inflation calculus globally. Lower energy prices reduce headline CPI, ease pressure on transportation costs, and could actually give the Fed more breathing room than the hawkish dot plot implies. The irony is that the Iran deal may be the single most powerful disinflationary event of 2026.

Today is also the last trading session before Juneteenth, with US markets closed Friday. That means any institutional repositioning — particularly given that June 19 would normally be a triple witching expiration Friday — was front-loaded into Thursday's session, adding volume and potential volatility to an already news-heavy close.

The 10-year vs. 2-year Treasury spread stands at +29 basis points as of June 17, a positively sloped curve that reflects a cautious growth outlook and persistent inflation — consistent with the Fed's reluctance to cut. The 30-year Treasury yield hit 4.93% this week, a level that historically pressures mortgage markets and long-duration equity valuations.

04 — What It Means

Breaking It Down: Rates, Stocks, the Economy, and What's Next

Rates & Treasury Yields

The 10-year Treasury yield closed Wednesday at 4.49% — its highest level since early June — after the hawkish FOMC outcome. The 2-year yield surged to 4.19%, compressing the 2s/10s spread to roughly +29 basis points. The curve is positively sloped but narrow, consistent with a "higher for longer" scenario where the Fed has removed its easing promise. The 30-year at 4.93% is particularly significant for mortgage markets and wealth management asset allocation. RSM now sees the September FOMC as a live meeting where the committee could hike for the first time since 2023.

Stocks & Indexes

Wednesday's 1.2% S&P 500 decline was the index's worst single-day Fed response for a new chair's debut since 1994. Thursday's partial recovery — driven by Intel's 10%+ surge and oil-related relief — brought the S&P back to roughly 7,506. The broader concern is that 58% of S&P 500 stocks are trading above their 50-day moving average, below the 70–80% levels seen earlier in the year, signaling that the recent rally has been narrower than it looks. Intel's 228% YTD gain and SpaceX's debut are outlier moves; the average stock has had a harder 2026.

Broader Economy

The macroeconomic backdrop going into the summer is genuinely mixed. On the positive side: the Iran peace deal is a substantial disinflationary shock, with oil now down 38% from April highs. On the negative side: core inflation remains well above the Fed's 2% target at around 4.2%, the May PPI components suggest a firm PCE print is coming, and the strong May jobs report (+172,000 vs. 80,000 expected) reflects a labor market that gives the Fed little justification to ease. The Beige Book described consumer spending as increasingly bifurcated, with middle-income households "squeezing more life out of every dollar."

What to Watch

Five upcoming catalysts deserve close attention: (1) May PCE price index, due end of June — the key input for the September FOMC decision. (2) Intel Q2 earnings, July 23 — first real read on Intel Foundry external revenue. (3) Strait of Hormuz reopening pace and actual tanker traffic — determines how fast Middle East supply normalizes. (4) Micron (MU) and FedEx (FDX) earnings next week, both sensitive to AI demand and freight/logistics trends. (5) Q1 GDP revision, also due end of month — will contextualize whether the economy is strong enough to absorb a potential hike.

Key Risk: September FOMC — The First Live Hike Decision Under Warsh

With 9 of 18 FOMC participants now projecting a 2026 rate hike and the easing bias officially gone from the statement, the September meeting is the first genuine decision point. The median dot now shows the fed funds rate ending 2026 at 3.8% — a 25 basis point hike from today's 3.50–3.75% range. What happens between now and September determines whether that hike materializes: if PCE comes in firm and the labor market stays strong, the committee has cover to move. If the Iran deal pushes oil lower and inflation cools meaningfully, the holds-camp retains the upper hand. Either way, markets will be pricing this binary for the next 90 days.

05 — Market Sector Lens

Who's Moving and Why on the Last Session Before the Long Weekend

Semiconductors / Technology Winning

Intel's 10%+ surge on the Trump-announced Apple chip deal lifted the broader semiconductor complex on Thursday, partially reversing Wednesday's selloff. Intel's 18A-P process node entering risk production validated the foundry thesis. Nvidia and AMD stabilized after being among the worst performers earlier this week. The Intel-Apple news is the most significant commercial win for U.S. domestic semiconductor manufacturing in years and could accelerate further supply chain reshoring conversations across the sector.

Energy Losing

Energy is the week's clear sector loser. WTI crude fell to $75.83 — down roughly 38% from its April conflict-peak — as the US-Iran MOU took effect and the Strait of Hormuz reopening began. The IEA projected a potential global supply surplus of 6 million barrels per day by 2027. Goldman Sachs moved its Gulf export normalization estimate to end of July. Integrated oil companies, refiners, and oilfield services names all face a structurally changed pricing backdrop over the next 60–90 days.

Rates-Sensitive Growth / Long Duration Losing

Wednesday's 7 basis point spike in the 10-year yield hit long-duration growth stocks hardest. The SaaS, fintech, and speculative tech segments saw the sharpest Wednesday declines. Higher discount rates mechanically compress the present value of distant cash flows, and with the Fed's easing bias now officially removed, the structural support that had anchored growth stock P/E expansion since the tariff recovery is weaker. Utilities and REITs also underperformed given their yield-proxy characteristics in a rising-rate environment.

Consumer Staples / Defensives Watch

Defensives held up better than growth amid Wednesday's hawkish shock, continuing a rotation pattern visible since early June. Lower oil prices are a net positive for consumer staples margins — fuel costs in logistics and packaging are significant inputs. However, these sectors face their own headwind from a higher-for-longer rate environment, as their bond-proxy characteristics make them less attractive when yields are elevated. Watch whether lower energy prices are enough to offset the rate headwind in the second half.

For everyone in finance

This week crystallized a concept every finance professional needs to internalize: the Fed is not just a rate-setting body — it is a communication machine, and how it communicates is itself a policy variable. Warsh's decision to strip forward guidance and decline to submit a dot changed market expectations as much as any actual rate move would have. Understanding the difference between a rate action and a communication action is a sign of genuine sophistication in any finance interview or client conversation.

Your HNW clients are asking two questions right now: (1) Is this rally sustainable? (2) Should I be reducing duration in my fixed income portfolio? The honest answer to the first question is "narrower than it looks" — 58% breadth vs. 80% earlier in 2026. For clients in the 55+ bracket with significant bond allocations, the 4.49% 10-year is the most attractive entry point since early March. The Iran peace deal is a genuine windfall for energy-importing economies and consumer spending power — that's a wealth-preservation story, not just a trading story.

Investment Banking

07 — Why This Matters for Interviews This Week

What to Actually Use in a Conversation

  • On the Fed: Separate the rate decision from the communication decision. The sophisticated answer isn't "the Fed held rates." It's: "Warsh's first meeting was actually a communication overhaul — he stripped the easing bias, declined to submit a dot, and let the committee's projections do the hawkish work for him. The policy rate didn't move, but the market's expected path of rates shifted meaningfully. That's how communication IS monetary policy."
  • On the dot plot: Know what it is and what it isn't. The dot plot shows where each FOMC participant thinks rates should be — not where they will be. Nine of 18 participants penciled in a hike, which moves the median year-end projection from 3.4% to 3.8%. But Warsh himself has publicly questioned the dot plot's value. Interviewers will respect candidates who can explain the tension between a tool Warsh distrusts but that the committee still published.
  • On Intel: Understand the foundry business model before praising the stock. Intel is simultaneously a chip designer AND a manufacturer (foundry). Most competitors — including Apple — use TSMC because it's better. If Intel wins Apple's business, it's commercial proof that the 18A node is competitive. But analyst targets are $82–$93 on a $121 stock. Demonstrating awareness of that gap — and what would need to be true for the stock to be worth the current price — shows genuine analytical depth.
  • On the Iran deal: Connect geopolitics to inflation to monetary policy. The Strait of Hormuz had been blocking ~14 million barrels per day of supply. Oil is down 38% from April highs. Lower oil = lower headline CPI = potentially less pressure on the Fed to hike. The elegant insight is that the Iran MOU and the Warsh hawkish dot plot are actually pulling in opposite directions on the inflation outlook — and markets are trying to price the net effect in real time.
  • On portfolio construction: The 4.49% 10-year yield is a real allocation question. For wealth management interviews, be ready to discuss why a 4.49% risk-free rate changes the denominator in every valuation, raises the hurdle rate for equity risk premium, and makes a 60/40 portfolio genuinely competitive again in ways it hasn't been for years. That's the strategic framing HNW advisors are actually having with clients right now.
08 — Term of the Day
The Dot Plot (Summary of Economic Projections)

The dot plot is the Federal Reserve's quarterly publication of individual FOMC participants' forecasts for the future path of the federal funds rate. Each participant submits their projection for where they believe the rate should be at year-end for the current year, one year out, two years out, and in the "longer run." The chart plots these projections as individual dots — hence the name. The median dot is the figure most widely cited by markets as the Fed's "signal" on future rate direction.

Today's issue makes the dot plot essential knowledge. The June 2026 dot plot revised the median year-end fed funds rate to 3.8% from 3.4% — a single 25 basis point hike baked into the committee's central projection. More importantly, 9 of 18 participants placed their dot above the current 3.50–3.75% range, meaning a majority of the committee thinks a hike is warranted before year-end. That single shift caused the S&P 500 to drop 1.2% and Treasury yields to spike on Wednesday.

Here's where it gets interesting for 2026: Chair Warsh is a well-documented critic of the dot plot. He declined to submit his own projection, and at his press conference he suggested the tool could "distort the market's price discovery function." He has hinted at a task force to review it. So you have a situation where the tool Warsh wants to potentially dismantle is simultaneously the tool that just delivered his hawkish message to markets. If Warsh succeeds in eventually eliminating or reforming the dot plot, the Fed loses one of its primary communication channels — and markets would need to find new signals for policy direction.

For wealth management professionals: the dot plot matters for fixed income duration decisions. When dots shift hawkish, bond prices fall (yields rise) and long-duration allocations underperform. When dots shift dovish, the opposite occurs. Understanding the mechanism — and its limitations — is exactly the kind of nuance that distinguishes a capable advisor from someone just reading Bloomberg headlines.
