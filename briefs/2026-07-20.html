The PHLX Semiconductor Index dropped nearly 10% last week — its worst stretch since April 2025 — dragging the S&P 500 down 1.6% and the Nasdaq down 2.9%. The trigger: Chinese AI startup Moonshot AI unveiled a model it claims narrows the gap with leading U.S. offerings, reigniting fears that AI hyperscalers could scale back infrastructure spending. The bulls need a reason to buy the dip, and they might get one: Tesla and Alphabet both report this week, and futures are already pointing green.

00 Smart in 30 Seconds
The whole issue in a paragraph

Friday's close left the S&P 500 at 7,457.69 (−1.01%) and the Nasdaq at 25,520.24 (−1.40%) after the PHLX Semiconductor Index shed nearly 10% for the week — its worst weekly performance since April 2025 — following Chinese startup Moonshot AI's release of a model it claims rivals top U.S. AI offerings, stoking fears that hyperscaler capex on chips could slow. The VIX spiked 12.2% to 18.77, WTI crude surged 4.5% to $82.47 on the back of a U.S.-Iran conflict now in its ninth day, and the 10-year Treasury yield dipped to 4.54% as investors sought safety. Monday morning brings relief: S&P 500 futures are up 0.6% and Nasdaq futures are up 1.1% as semis attempt a bounce — but the real stress test is mid-week, when Tesla and Alphabet report earnings and investors look for hard data on AI monetization and capital spending plans.

01 What Moved Markets

A Chinese AI Model Torched the Chip Sector. Oil Piled On.

Semiconductors

Chinese AI startup Moonshot AI released its Kimi K3 model on Friday, which it described as narrowing the gap with leading U.S. AI offerings. The announcement reignited the core fear behind every chip selloff this year: if smaller, cheaper models can match frontier AI performance, hyperscalers may not need to keep expanding their GPU infrastructure at the current pace. Nvidia fell 2.2%, Broadcom shed 1%, AMD dropped 1%, and Intel retreated 2%. The VanEck Semiconductor ETF (SMH) posted its third weekly decline in four weeks, falling nearly 9% for the period.

Geopolitics & Energy

WTI crude surged roughly 4.5% to $82.47 per barrel as the U.S.-Iran conflict entered its ninth day since a ceasefire collapsed, pushing gas prices in the U.S. back above the $4-per-gallon threshold. Higher energy prices are a double-edged problem: they lift energy stocks but add to input costs across the economy, feeding the inflationary pressures the Fed is already watching closely. Cleveland Fed President Beth Hammack said Friday that she is hearing from business leaders that the central bank may need to take action on inflation — stopping short of endorsing a hike, but doing nothing to calm bond markets.

Earnings Movers

Travelers Companies surged over 9% after posting $10.04 in earnings per share against a Wall Street consensus of just $5.41 — one of the largest beats of the season so far. On the other end, Netflix sank 7.3% after forecasting another quarter of slowing sales growth, and Intuitive Surgical fell 10% despite beating estimates, as investors punished management's commentary linking the expiration of ACA premium subsidies to softer robotic surgery procedure volumes.

02 Why It Mattered

This Isn't Just a Chip Story. It's a Question About the Whole AI Trade.

The Moonshot AI release landed at the worst possible moment for a market that had bet heavily on AI infrastructure spending being a multi-year, near-inelastic growth driver. The logic behind the 2025–2026 chip boom was simple: frontier AI requires ever-more-powerful chips, and only a handful of companies — Nvidia above all — could supply them. Every time a new, efficient model emerges from China at a fraction of the cost, that logic wobbles.

What makes this iteration especially unsettling is that Moonshot's Kimi K3 followed closely on the heels of Alibaba's Qwen 3.8 Max release, which Alibaba described as second only to Anthropic's latest model. Two major Chinese AI model releases in the span of days raises the question markets have been reluctant to ask directly: is the U.S. AI efficiency advantage narrowing faster than Wall Street priced in? If yes, the downstream impact on Nvidia's forward revenue estimates — and by extension the entire semiconductor supply chain — is material.

Overlay the geopolitical risk: the U.S.-Iran conflict is keeping oil elevated, which is complicating the Fed's calculus ahead of its July 28–29 meeting. The FOMC held rates at 3.50%–3.75% in June, but nine officials already projected at least one rate hike in 2026. A market simultaneously pricing chip-capex risk and a possible rate hike is a difficult environment for growth stocks to recover in. Something has to give — and earnings from Tesla and Alphabet this week may be the catalyst that determines which direction.

Note: The FOMC's next meeting is July 28–29. Markets are watching energy and wage data closely for signs that the Fed's higher-for-longer posture could tip into an outright hike before year-end.

04 What It Means

Four Corners of a Market at an Inflection Point

For Rates

The 10-year Treasury dipped to 4.54% Friday as a partial flight to safety. But Cleveland Fed President Hammack's inflation comments and persistent energy price pressure keep a July or September rate hike on the table. The FOMC meeting July 28–29 is now a live event — not a foregone hold. Watch the 10-year: a sustained move above 4.7% would stress equity valuations further.

For Equities

Futures are bouncing Monday — S&P up 0.6%, Nasdaq up 1.1% — suggesting dip-buyers are active after last week's carnage. But the recovery is fragile. If Tesla and Alphabet disappoint on AI capex or revenue guidance, the bounce will likely fade fast. The market needs earnings, not just cheap prices, to sustain a recovery from these levels.

For the Economy

Gas prices breaching $4 per gallon again is a consumer confidence hit and an inflation signal simultaneously. Higher fuel costs tend to pass through to transportation, manufacturing, and food within 4–6 weeks. If oil holds above $80 into August, the June CPI print (which surprised to the downside) may look like a head-fake rather than a trend.

What to Watch

Tesla reports mid-week: delivery guidance and any commentary on energy storage or autonomous revenue will be the key read-through. Alphabet follows: cloud growth rate and AI product monetization are the metrics that matter. ServiceNow, IBM, Intel, and Texas Instruments also report — together they form a comprehensive picture of enterprise AI spending in Q2.

Key Watch Item

Alphabet's capex guidance. If Alphabet signals any pullback in data center or chip spending, it will validate the Moonshot AI fear and likely extend the semiconductor selloff. If it raises guidance, the AI infrastructure thesis gets a reprieve — and chip stocks could stage a meaningful recovery into month-end.

06 Finance Career Lens

What This Week's Market Tells Recruiters You Need to Know

Across all tracks

This is one of those weeks where understanding the connection between AI model efficiency, chip capex, and equity valuation is table stakes. If you can articulate why a cheaper AI model from China sends Nvidia lower, you've already demonstrated more market literacy than most candidates in the room.

Wealth Management & Private Banking

High-net-worth clients who loaded up on Mag 7 and semiconductor names in the first half of 2026 are sitting on drawdowns. The conversation this week is about rebalancing: do you trim into any bounce, or hold through earnings? Understanding how to frame the AI capex narrative and geopolitical risk to a client — without creating unnecessary panic — is a core skill interviewers will probe. Think in terms of portfolio construction, not stock tips.

Equity Research

Moonshot AI's Kimi K3 release is exactly the kind of event that requires an analyst to update their model assumptions in real time. The key question: does this change your revenue estimates for Nvidia's data center segment, or is this noise? Research candidates should be ready to discuss how they'd assess a competitive threat from a private Chinese AI company with limited financial disclosure — and what primary research channels (channel checks, management commentary, patent filings) they'd use to form a view.

Investment Banking

Brookfield and CPP Investments agreed this morning to acquire LXP Industrial Trust in a $5.2 billion all-cash deal, taking the industrial REIT private. That's a live M&A transaction to study: structure, premium, rationale, and financing. Meanwhile, the IPO and deal pipeline is sensitive to equity market volatility — a sustained chip selloff raises the cost of equity and can delay tech-sector transactions. Expect interview questions this cycle to center on how market dislocations affect deal timing and valuation multiples.

07 Why This Matters for Interviews

The Questions You'll Get This Week — and How to Answer Them

  • "Walk me through what happened to semiconductor stocks last week." — This is your chance to show macro-to-micro fluency. Start with the Moonshot AI model release, connect it to hyperscaler capex fears, name the stocks and their moves, reference the PHLX SOX entering bear-market territory, and tie it to the broader Nasdaq decline. Numbers matter: SOX −10% for the week, Nvidia −2.2% on Friday, SMH third weekly loss in four weeks.
  • What a strong candidate says: "The selloff wasn't purely about Kimi K3 in isolation — it was about whether efficient open-source AI models from China structurally reduce the amount of compute U.S. hyperscalers need to buy. If the answer is yes even partially, the forward earnings estimates for the semiconductor supply chain look too optimistic."
  • Follow-up angle: "How does the Fed factor into the chip recovery trade?" Answer: If the FOMC signals a hike at its July 28–29 meeting, discount rates rise and growth stock valuations compress — making a near-term chip recovery harder even if earnings are fine.
  • Do NOT say: "Chips fell because of China." That's too vague. The specific mechanism — efficient AI models reducing capex demand — is what separates a well-prepared candidate from someone who just read a headline.
08 Term of the Day
Hyperscaler Capex

Capital expenditure by the largest cloud and AI infrastructure companies — think Microsoft, Google, Amazon, and Meta — on data centers, servers, and chips. This spending is the primary demand driver for semiconductor companies like Nvidia. When investors fear that hyperscalers will slow or optimize their chip purchases (as they might if efficient AI models require less raw compute), semiconductor stocks sell off sharply. Last week's Moonshot AI release was a direct threat to hyperscaler capex assumptions: if a Chinese startup can match frontier AI performance with a more efficient architecture, the justification for buying ever-more GPUs weakens. Alphabet's earnings this week will provide the most direct read on whether hyperscaler capex is actually slowing — or whether the fear is overblown.
