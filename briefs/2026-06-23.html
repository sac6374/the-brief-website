The AI Trade Cracks: Chips Crater, BofA Calls Three Fed Hikes

The semiconductor sector had its worst day in months. South Korea's Kospi crashed 10%, triggering circuit breakers in Seoul — and the panic spread fast, slamming U.S. chip stocks and pulling the Nasdaq down 2.2%. Bank of America poured fuel on the fire, calling for three consecutive Fed rate hikes before year-end. The combination was enough to erase roughly $1 trillion in Nasdaq 100 market value in a single session.

00 Smart in 30 Seconds
The whole issue in a paragraph

South Korea's Kospi collapsed 10% Tuesday — triggering exchange circuit breakers in Seoul — as memory chip giants SK Hynix and Samsung each shed more than 12%, unleashing a wave of selling that swept into U.S. markets and knocked the Nasdaq down 2.21% and the S&P 500 down 1.44%. Stateside, Micron fell 13%, SanDisk dropped 12%, and the VanEck Semiconductor ETF (SMH) lost 6.5%, briefly wiping roughly $1 trillion in Nasdaq 100 market value. Bank of America escalated the damage by publishing a hawkish rate note calling for three consecutive 25bp Fed hikes — in September, October, and December — lifting the funds rate to 4.25%–4.50%, the most aggressive call on Wall Street. On the bright side, the Dow held nearly flat as defensive stocks and industrials caught a bid, WTI crude slid to ~$73/barrel as U.S.–Iran diplomacy advanced, and FedEx reported a blowout quarter after the bell — $6.31 adjusted EPS versus a $5.96 consensus estimate — in its debut as a standalone parcel company.

01 What Moved Markets Today

Chips Crashed Globally, BofA Called Three Hikes, and FedEx Delivered a Beat

Global Chip Rout

South Korea's Kospi benchmark plunged 10% on Tuesday, triggering circuit breakers on the Korea Exchange as heavyweight chipmakers SK Hynix and Samsung each fell more than 12%. The selloff — rooted in AI-spending fatigue and valuation concerns following Broadcom's guidance miss earlier this month — ripped through U.S. markets: Micron dropped 13%, SanDisk fell 12%, and the VanEck SMH semiconductor ETF shed 6.5% to $625.62. The Philadelphia Semiconductor Index tumbled 7.3%.

BofA Rate Shock

Bank of America economist Aditya Bhave published a note calling for three consecutive 25bp Fed rate hikes in September, October, and December 2026, bringing the federal funds rate to 4.25%–4.50% — roughly 25 basis points above what the market currently prices. BofA had previously expected cuts in 2026; the reversal adds to a growing hawkish chorus that now includes Deutsche Bank (two hikes), BNP Paribas, and Macquarie. CME FedWatch puts September hike probability at 72.8%.

FedEx Earnings Beat

After the closing bell, FedEx reported Q4 fiscal 2026 adjusted EPS of $6.31 versus the $5.96 consensus estimate, on revenue of $25 billion against a $24.04 billion expectation. This was the logistics giant's first quarterly report as a pure-play parcel and express company, following the June 1 spinoff of FedEx Freight. The company also announced intentions to repurchase up to $1 billion in shares opportunistically by fiscal year-end.

Oil & Geopolitics

WTI crude fell to around $73.10/barrel, its lowest level in nearly three months, after Washington issued a 60-day license allowing Iran to sell oil on international markets as U.S.–Iran peace negotiations showed constructive progress. Traffic through the Strait of Hormuz has been picking up, with Kuwait and the UAE using alternative shipping routes and Iran exporting more than 30 million barrels in the past week. The 10-year Treasury yield slipped to 4.50% as a flight-to-safety bid accompanied the equity selloff.

02 Why It Mattered

The AI Rally's Most Crowded Trade Just Got a Margin Call From the Market

Semiconductors didn't just fall Tuesday — they broke a narrative. For most of 2026, chip stocks were the engine of the equity rally. The argument was simple: AI capex was accelerating, data center demand for memory and logic chips was insatiable, and earnings were proving it. Micron had just hit a record close on Monday. Then Seoul opened.

The Korea selloff wasn't random noise. It reflected something investors have been quietly worried about for weeks: the AI spending cycle may be running ahead of actual monetizable demand. When Broadcom reported strong results earlier this month but failed to raise its full-year AI chip guidance, it planted a seed of doubt. Tuesday, that seed sprouted globally. The Kospi circuit break made it visceral — and circuit breakers have a way of reminding markets that panic is contagious.

Then BofA dropped its rate call. Three hikes. That's not just a forecast revision — it's a direct challenge to the valuation math underpinning every AI-linked growth stock. High-multiple tech names are priced off discounted future cash flows; when the discount rate rises, those valuations compress mechanically. The fact that 60% of the S&P 500 finished in the green today — with defensive stocks like Walmart and Johnson & Johnson moving higher — tells you investors didn't abandon equities. They rotated out of the trade that had gotten too crowded.

Wedbush's Dan Ives characterized the move as a pullback after a powerful rally, not a structural break. Micron earnings — due Wednesday after the close — will be the next real test of whether the AI thesis is intact or impaired.

04 What It Means

Three Hikes, Compressed Multiples, and a Memory Market Under Scrutiny

For Rates

If BofA's three-hike call gains traction, the 10-year yield — currently at 4.50% — could push toward 5%. That would reset the risk-free rate used in DCF models across the market, compressing P/E multiples on growth stocks most aggressively. CME FedWatch already prices a 72.8% chance of a September hike and 87.9% for December.

For Equities

Breadth held up surprisingly well today — 299 of 503 S&P 500 holdings finished green — which suggests this was a sector rotation, not a market breakdown. But if the AI-spending thesis cracks further in Micron's earnings, the rotation could become a rout. Watch whether FAANG and mega-cap tech can hold their support levels.

For the Economy

Falling oil (WTI at ~$73) provides a natural inflation tailwind as U.S.–Iran talks progress. But BofA's call implies that core PCE remains sticky at 3.5%, well above the Fed's 2% target. That tension — cheaper energy vs. elevated services inflation — is the central macro puzzle of the second half of 2026.

What to Watch

Micron earnings Wednesday, PCE Friday. Any guidance downgrade from Micron on HBM (high-bandwidth memory) demand would directly validate Seoul's panic. Conversely, a beat-and-raise from Micron could quickly reverse Tuesday's chip sector losses. Also watch whether Fed officials push back on or validate BofA's hike timetable in public remarks this week.

Key Watch Item

Micron's Q3 FY2026 earnings after Wednesday's close are the single most important near-term catalyst. Memory is the backbone of AI inference at scale. If Micron guides HBM demand lower, the AI trade faces a fundamental — not just valuation — challenge.

05 Market Sector Lens

Tech Bled, Defensives Caught a Bid, Energy Gave Ground to Diplomacy

HEALTH CARE +~1%

Investors rotated hard into defensives. Johnson & Johnson and Merck led the sector higher as traders sought shelter from tech volatility. IBM — technically a legacy tech name — surged 5% on a JPMorgan upgrade to overweight, bucking the sector trend.

TECHNOLOGY −4.1%

The S&P 500 tech sector fell 4.13% — the single biggest drag on the index. Semiconductors led losses across the board. Micron, SanDisk, Qualcomm (−8.6%), AMD (−7%+), and Arm Holdings (−8.4%) drove the damage. Even Nvidia — the year's biggest winner — shed more than 3%.

ENERGY Watch

WTI crude slid to ~$73/barrel on Iran deal progress, pressuring energy stocks even as broader defensive names rallied. The IEA has also cut its 2026 global demand growth forecast. Lower oil is disinflationary — good for the Fed's calculus — but a headwind for energy sector earnings. Watch OPEC+ response in coming weeks.

06 Finance Career Lens

What Tuesday's Selloff Reveals About How Finance Professionals Think

Across all tracks

Days like Tuesday test one core skill: distinguishing between a sector rotation and a structural breakdown. The answer — and how you frame it — signals market sophistication to any interviewer or client.

Wealth Management & Private Banking

Clients with concentrated tech and semiconductor positions just had a bad day. The conversation shifts immediately to rebalancing, diversification, and whether the AI thesis is a tactical trade or a long-term structural holding. This is exactly when advisors earn their fees: helping clients separate emotion from portfolio logic. The fact that 60% of S&P 500 names finished green today is a useful anchor in that conversation.

Equity Research

Semiconductor analysts will be working late tonight. Revising price targets, updating earnings models, and fielding calls from buy-side clients who want to know if Tuesday was a buying opportunity or a warning sign. The Micron earnings Wednesday are the crux — your channel checks on HBM demand and data center capex commentary from hyperscalers matter more than ever right now.

Investment Banking

BofA's three-hike call has direct implications for deal markets. Higher rates compress LBO returns, raise hurdle rates for M&A, and reduce the present value of synergies. If the September hike expectation firms up, expect acquirers to demand lower multiples and sellers to resist. Sponsors already pricing in cuts will need to rerun their models. Watch credit spreads — they're the canary.

07 Why This Matters for Interviews

The Semiconductor Selloff Question Is Already in Your Next Interview

  • They'll ask: "Walk me through what happened in chip stocks today and why it matters for the broader market."
  • Strong answer: Start with the Seoul circuit break as the trigger, explain the transmission mechanism (global risk-off, AI valuation concerns, BofA rate call amplifying pressure on high-multiple names), note the rotation into defensives as evidence of sector repositioning rather than panic selling, and flag Micron earnings as the next binary catalyst.
  • Follow-up angle: "How does BofA's three-hike forecast change your view on semiconductor valuations?" — Be ready to discuss how rising discount rates mechanically compress the present value of future earnings, and why high-P/E tech is disproportionately exposed.
  • Don't say: "The market crashed today." It didn't — 60% of S&P 500 names closed green. Precision matters. Say "the tech sector sold off sharply while defensives and select industrials rotated higher."
08 Term of the Day
Circuit Breaker

A circuit breaker is an automatic mechanism that temporarily halts trading on an exchange when prices move beyond a pre-set threshold — designed to curb panic selling and give investors time to process information rationally. South Korea's Korea Exchange activated its circuit breaker Tuesday when the Kospi plunged 10%, briefly suspending trading for 20 minutes. In the U.S., the S&P 500 triggers a 15-minute halt at −7% (Level 1) and −13% (Level 2), with trading halted entirely for the day at −20% (Level 3).

09 Say This Today

Sound Sharp in Any Finance Room

In a conversation about today's selloff

"Today wasn't a market selloff — it was a sector repricing. Seven of eleven S&P 500 sectors finished positive. What happened is that the AI trade, which had gotten extremely crowded in semiconductors, hit two catalysts simultaneously: a 10% crash in Korea's Kospi that signaled global AI-capex skepticism, and a Bank of America note calling for three Fed hikes before year-end. When your most crowded trade collides with a rising discount rate, the math gets ugly fast. The real tell is Wednesday — Micron's earnings will either validate the fear or kill it."
