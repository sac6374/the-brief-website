Micron Posts $41.5B Quarter — And the AI Trade Lives Again

Wall Street spent the day in holding-pattern mode, waiting for Micron to either confirm or kill the AI memory trade after a brutal 48-hour chip selloff. The verdict arrived after the bell: Micron absolutely crushed it — $41.5B in revenue versus the $35.6B consensus, EPS of $25.11 versus the $20.60 expected — and guided Q4 to $50B, nearly $7B above what analysts had penciled in. Meanwhile, oil slid to its lowest level since before the Iran conflict began, 10-year yields dipped below 4.5%, and the Dow was the only major index to close green on the day.

00 Smart in 30 Seconds
The whole issue in a paragraph

Markets closed mixed and jittery on Wednesday as investors held their breath for Micron's Q3 earnings — and Micron delivered in spectacular fashion after the bell: $41.5B in revenue (vs. $35.6B expected), EPS of $25.11 (vs. $20.60 expected), and Q4 revenue guidance of $50B against a $42.9B consensus. The VanEck Semiconductor ETF jumped 3% in after-hours trading and the Roundhill Memory ETF surged 10%. During the regular session, the Nasdaq fell 0.43% and the S&P 500 slipped 0.10% while the Dow gained 0.35%, with a 4% oil crash pulling the 10-year yield below 4.5% as Iran peace talks advanced. The backdrop remains hawkish: BofA now expects three Fed rate hikes in 2026 under new Chair Kevin Warsh, and markets are pricing a 68% chance of a September hike — up from 29% last week.

01 What Moved Markets Today

Three Forces: Micron's Earnings, Crashing Oil, and a Hawkish Fed Overhang

Micron Earnings (After-Hours)

Micron Technology (MU) reported Q3 2026 fiscal results after the close that obliterated expectations on every meaningful line. Revenue came in at $41.456B — a 346% year-over-year increase and a massive beat versus the $35.59B consensus. Adjusted EPS hit $25.11 versus the $20.60 expected. Q4 guidance of $49–$51B in revenue nearly $7B above what analysts were modeling sent the stock up more than 6% in after-hours trade, with the VanEck Semiconductor ETF (SMH) rising 3% and the Roundhill Memory ETF (DRAM) surging 10% in sympathy.

Oil Crash / Iran Diplomacy

WTI crude fell 3.92% to settle at $70.34 per barrel — briefly dipping below $70 for the first time since early March — as tankers resumed transit through the Strait of Hormuz and U.S.-Iran peace talks advanced. Brent crude dropped 4.33% to $73.74 per barrel, its lowest since before U.S. and Israeli airstrikes on Iran began in late February. President Trump confirmed Iran would impose no tolls or insurance charges on ships traversing the strait, removing a key source of energy-market anxiety. The oil collapse pulled the 10-year Treasury yield below 4.5%, as lower energy prices reduce near-term inflation pressure.

Fed Rate-Hike Fears

The market is still digesting last week's hawkish debut from new Fed Chair Kevin Warsh, who held rates steady at 3.50%–3.75% on June 17 but stripped the FOMC statement of its easing language and oversaw an inflation forecast upgrade to 3.6% headline and 3.3% core for 2026. Bank of America now forecasts three 25-basis-point hikes in September, October, and December. Markets are pricing a September hike probability of 68%, up sharply from 29% before the June meeting. Senator Elizabeth Warren appeared on CNBC Wednesday to say the Fed is caught in a policy box of the administration's making.

02 Why It Mattered

Micron Was the Only Thing Standing Between the AI Trade and a Full Breakdown

The chip sector had been on life support for 72 hours. The rout started Monday when Alphabet plunged on AI talent concerns, accelerated Tuesday when South Korea's KOSPI chip index fell nearly 10%, and arrived on U.S. shores as Micron, Nvidia, AMD, Qualcomm, and Sandisk each fell between 4% and 13%. The Philadelphia Semiconductor Index (SOX) dropped 7.9% on Tuesday alone — one of its sharpest single-day declines of the year. The bear case was straightforward: if AI hyperscalers like Microsoft, Meta, and Google were spending furiously on data centers but couldn't show returns, and if SK Hynix was slowing HBM production to pivot back to commodity DRAM, then the entire AI memory supercycle narrative was suspect.

Enter Micron. A revenue print of $41.5B when the street expected $35.6B, combined with a Q4 guide of $50B — nearly 40% above the same quarter a year ago — is not a slowdown. CEO Sanjay Mehrotra's message on the earnings call was unambiguous: memory market tightness is "locked in to persist beyond calendar 2027" amid the AI buildout and the industry-wide pivot toward High Bandwidth Memory (HBM). That one line reframes the entire week's selloff as a sentiment overreaction rather than a structural crack in the AI trade.

But the macro backdrop is not without risk. The Fed's hawkish pivot — with BofA forecasting three hikes before year-end — keeps the cost of capital elevated for capital-intensive chipmakers running massive capex programs. And the oil collapse, while good for inflation, is destroying energy-sector earnings and weighing on the broader market's composition. For now, the bulls have their data point. Whether they hold it into next week's PCE report is another question entirely.

Context: Micron shares had gained more than 300% year-to-date through their Monday all-time high, making Tuesday's 13% drop a painful but not surprising recalibration for an overextended leader.
03 The Chain Reaction

From Korean Chip Crash to Micron Blowout: How the Week Unfolded

Trigger
South Korea's KOSPI chip index plunged ~10% on Tuesday as SK Hynix slowed HBM production; BofA issued a note forecasting three Fed rate hikes; Broadcom's prior earnings guidance had disappointed. Micron fell 13%, Sandisk 11%, Qualcomm 8%, AMD 5.8%, Nvidia 4.2% on Tuesday.
Immediate
The Nasdaq fell 2.21% and S&P 500 lost 1.44% on Tuesday. The Philadelphia SOX index dropped 7.9%. The VIX spiked 12.8% to 19.49. Gold dropped below $4,000 for the first time since November 2025. Defensive names — Walmart, J&J, Coca-Cola — rallied as investors rotated out of tech.
2nd Order
Wednesday opened with a partial recovery as futures pointed higher on Micron earnings optimism. Oil crashed 4% on Iran progress, pulling 10-year yields below 4.5%. The Dow gained 0.35% as industrials and defensives held up, but the Nasdaq still closed down 0.43% as chip stocks couldn't fully bounce ahead of the after-hours report. After the bell, Micron's blowout print sent SMH up 3% and DRAM up 10% in extended hours.
Watch
Thursday's Qualcomm Investor Day is the next catalyst. Friday brings the May PCE inflation report — the Fed's preferred gauge — which will directly test whether BofA's three-hike forecast is too aggressive or not aggressive enough. Micron's after-hours move will also set the tone for Thursday's regular open.
04 What It Means

Cheap Oil, Expensive Hikes, and a Memory Market That Won't Quit

For Rates

The 10-year yield dropped below 4.5% as oil prices collapsed — lower energy prices reduce near-term inflation pressure and give the Fed slightly more room. But the market is still pricing a 68% probability of a September hike. The June FOMC dot plot showed 9 of 18 participants expecting at least one hike this year. BofA and Deutsche Bank have already revised forecasts to include hikes. The PCE report on Friday is now the most important data release of the month.

For Equities

Micron's blowout restores confidence in the AI capex cycle — at least in memory and HBM. But broader tech remains under pressure from the rate-hike narrative. The S&P 500 Technology sector fell 4.13% on Tuesday alone. If the PCE print comes in hot on Friday, the rate-hike trade intensifies and growth stocks face renewed pressure. If it comes in cool, the relief rally in tech extends. The market is binary heading into the weekend.

For the Economy

Oil below $70 is unambiguously disinflationary and good for consumers and most businesses. But it is devastating for energy companies and reflects a rapid unwinding of the Iran war premium — a situation that could reverse quickly if talks collapse. On the growth side, the Fed's own updated projections show 2026 GDP growth at 2.2% — solid but not spectacular — with unemployment projected at 4.3%. The economy is not in recession, but the policy risk is real.

What to Watch

Friday's May PCE report is the single most important near-term event for rate expectations. Alphabet officially joins the Dow next Monday, further embedding mega-cap tech into the blue-chip index. Qualcomm's Investor Day on Thursday could extend the semiconductor recovery rally if guidance is strong. And the Fed's bank stress test results were also released today — watch for follow-through in financial sector names.

Key Watch Item

Friday May PCE inflation print: the Fed's preferred inflation gauge. A print above 3.3% core would validate BofA's three-hike forecast and likely reset equity valuations again. A print below 3.0% would give the bulls room to run into Q3 earnings season starting in July.

05 Market Sector Lens

Industrials and Defensives Held the Line While Tech Tried to Recover

CONSUMER STAPLES +1.8%

Walmart, Johnson & Johnson, and Coca-Cola all gained on Tuesday as investors rotated into defensives amid the tech selloff. Consumer staples are functioning as the market's shock absorber right now — the classic flight-to-safety rotation when growth names get stress-tested.

ENERGY −4%+

WTI crude's 3.92% single-day decline to $70.34 crushed energy names across the board. The sector is down roughly 40% from its wartime peak as the Iran war premium rapidly unwinds. With the Strait of Hormuz effectively reopening, energy stocks face a structural headwind until the supply picture stabilizes.

TECHNOLOGY / SEMICONDUCTORS Watch

The SMH ETF was down more than 5% on the week heading into Micron's print. After the blowout report, SMH jumped 3% in after-hours trade and the DRAM memory ETF surged 10%. Whether this translates to a full sector recovery on Thursday depends on how the market prices Micron's guidance relative to the hawkish rate backdrop. Arm Holdings rose 3% on analyst price target upgrades, offering a constructive data point within the group.

06 Finance Career Lens

This Week Is a Master Class in Risk Sentiment, Sector Rotation, and Earnings Analysis

Across all tracks

This week demonstrates exactly how quickly market sentiment can pivot — from a global chip rout to a blowout earnings print within 48 hours. Understanding the difference between a structural bear thesis and a positioning-driven correction is a core skill across every finance career path.

Wealth Management & Private Banking

Client conversations this week will inevitably turn to the chip selloff and whether to add exposure. The key framing: Tuesday's move was a sentiment-driven crowded-trade unwind, not a fundamental deterioration — Micron's $50B Q4 guide proves that. For clients with long-term AI exposure through broad tech ETFs, this week is noise. For those overweight semiconductors on a tactical basis, the risk/reward just improved materially after the blowout. Know the difference between rebalancing and panic-selling.

Equity Research

Micron's Q3 print is an analyst's dream case study: a massive revenue beat ($41.5B vs. $35.6B), earnings upside ($25.11 vs. $20.60), and forward guidance that resets the entire comparable set. Any analyst covering semiconductors, cloud infrastructure, or AI hardware now needs to revisit their model. The key output: does HBM demand tightness through 2027 justify current valuations, and how does a potential Fed rate hike in September change the discount rate assumption? Those are the two questions every sell-side note will need to answer by Friday morning.

Investment Banking

SK Hynix's reported plans for a nearly $30 billion U.S. listing is the IB story of the week that nobody is talking about because Micron drowned it out. That would be one of the largest foreign listings in U.S. history and would generate enormous equity capital markets fees. Watch for IPO pipeline commentary from the major banks next quarter. Meanwhile, the FedEx miss and Cerebras' 11% post-IPO earnings drop are reminders that not every recent market debut is bulletproof — valuation discipline matters even in a momentum market.

07 Why This Matters for Interviews

Be Ready to Talk Micron, the AI Capex Cycle, and the Fed's Policy Box

  • Question you might get: "Micron fell 13% on Tuesday and then surged after hours on earnings. How do you think about valuing a stock with that kind of volatility?" — A strong answer distinguishes between market sentiment (the Tuesday selloff was positioning-driven, not fundamental) and business performance (Q3 revenue up 346% YoY, Q4 guide of $50B). Frame it around intrinsic value vs. market price.
  • What a strong candidate says: "The selloff reflected crowded positioning and macro risk repricing — not a change in Micron's earnings power. The blowout Q3 results and $50B Q4 guide confirmed the HBM demand thesis. If anything, the pullback was an opportunity to revisit fair value with updated numbers."
  • Follow-up angle: "How does the Fed's hawkish pivot affect your semiconductor thesis?" — Answer: higher rates raise the discount rate, compressing multiples on high-growth names. But if earnings growth is fast enough — Micron's revenue is up 346% YoY — the earnings yield expansion can still outpace multiple compression. This is a fundamental valuation exercise.
  • Do NOT say: "Semiconductors are just too volatile right now" — that signals you don't understand the difference between price volatility and business risk, and it shows you haven't done the work.
08 Term of the Day
High Bandwidth Memory (HBM)

HBM is a type of DRAM architecture that stacks multiple memory chips vertically and connects them with ultra-fast pathways, dramatically increasing the speed at which data moves between memory and processor. It is the critical ingredient inside Nvidia's AI accelerator chips — every H100 and Blackwell GPU contains HBM — making it the bottleneck component of the entire AI infrastructure buildout. Micron CEO Sanjay Mehrotra said on tonight's earnings call that HBM market tightness is "locked in" well past 2027, which is why Micron's $50B Q4 guide was so stunning relative to expectations.
