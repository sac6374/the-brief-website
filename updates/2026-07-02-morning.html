Stocks Rally on Soft Jobs Print — Rate Hike Risk Fades, Chips Still Bleeding

A weaker-than-expected June nonfarm payrolls report — just 57,000 jobs added vs. 113,000 expected — has markets rallying this morning as investors bet the Fed under Chair Kevin Warsh has less ammunition to hike near term. The Dow hit a fresh all-time intraday high, even as a brutal overnight chip rout in Asia continued to weigh on semis. Meanwhile, a bombshell trade headline landed Wednesday: the Trump administration officially declined to renew USMCA, kicking off what could be a decade of rolling renegotiations with Mexico and Canada.

Key Points
  • JOBS MISS, BUT MARKET LIKES IT: June nonfarm payrolls came in at just 57,000 — well below the 113,000–115,000 consensus. The unemployment rate ticked down to 4.2% from 4.3%. The soft print takes near-term pressure off the Fed to hike, with the 2-year Treasury yield falling on the news. Markets are still pricing a 60%+ chance of a September hike, but today's number buys some breathing room. The Dow rallied over 390 points and hit a fresh all-time intraday high.
  • CHIP ROUT GOES GLOBAL: Wednesday's U.S. semi selloff — Micron -10.6%, AMD -6.9%, Intel -9% — spread overnight to Asia with devastating force. South Korea's KOSPI fell ~4% to around 7,990, dragged by Samsung Electronics (-6.7%) and SK Hynix (-8.4%). Hong Kong-listed chip names were obliterated, with SMIC down 11% and Chinese AI chip firms off 14–18%. The common thread: crowded positioning in AI memory names unwinding fast after a stellar first half. South Korea's inflation also hit 3.2% in June — a 2.5-year high — adding macro pressure.
  • USMCA IS NOT RENEWED: In a major trade development, the Trump administration officially declined to renew the U.S.-Mexico-Canada Agreement on its July 1 deadline. The pact remains in force — but now faces annual reviews over the next decade before a potential 2036 expiration. USTR Jamieson Greer said the U.S. is 'not prepared to rubber stamp' the deal, citing trade deficits with both neighbors. A third round of bilateral talks with Mexico is set for July 20 in Mexico City. Canada has largely been left out of negotiations amid ongoing tensions with PM Carney.
  • ALPHABET TAKES A HIT IN EUROPE: Shares of Alphabet fell over 1% after the Google parent lost its appeal against a record 4.1-billion-euro ($4.67B) EU fine stemming from a 2018 European Commission ruling on Android anticompetitive practices. Separately, Palantir shares jumped 4% after D.A. Davidson upgraded the AI defense software company to Buy, while SpaceX (SPCX) gained on Wedbush's initiation with an Outperform rating and $190 price target — just ahead of its scheduled Nasdaq 100 inclusion on July 7.
What to Watch

→ FED REACTION FUNCTION: With the June jobs print well below consensus, all eyes are on how Chair Warsh interprets the data. He has repeatedly told Wall Street to read the data, not the Fed — today's print is a test of that framework. Watch for any Fed speaker commentary throughout the day and whether rate futures reprice the September meeting meaningfully.

→ CHIP SECTOR STABILIZATION: Can semis find a floor? The AI-driven rotation out of chipmakers has been swift — Micron, AMD, Intel, Samsung, SK Hynix, and Hong Kong-listed names all took heavy losses. Watch Nvidia and Broadcom intraday for signs of whether this is a healthy profit-taking correction or the start of a broader AI valuation rethink heading into Q3 earnings season (banks kick off July 14).

→ USMCA FALLOUT AND SUPPLY CHAIN RISK: The non-renewal of USMCA introduces long-tail uncertainty for automakers, farmers, retailers, and energy companies relying on integrated North American supply chains. Watch CAT, GM, Ford, and MX/CAD currency pairs for early reactions. A third U.S.-Mexico negotiating round July 20 will be the next hard catalyst to watch.
