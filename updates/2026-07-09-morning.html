War Fog Lifts (Slightly): Markets Claw Back as AI Trumps Iran Anxiety

US stocks are edging higher Thursday morning as investors lean back into the AI trade and oil prices pull off their Wednesday spike highs, even as the US-Iran conflict keeps escalating. The US military struck roughly 90 targets across Iran overnight, and Tehran responded by hitting US-allied Gulf states — yet Wall Street is shrugging off the worst of it, at least for now. Buckle up: the situation on the ground is fluid, and Strait of Hormuz risk is nowhere near priced out.

Key Points
  • US-Iran war escalates: The US military struck around 90 targets inside Iran overnight Thursday, and Tehran retaliated by launching missiles at US-allied Gulf countries including sites in Bahrain and Kuwait. At least 14 people have been reported killed in US strikes over the past two days. The fighting was triggered after Iranian attacks on commercial vessels — including a Qatari LNG carrier and a Saudi oil tanker — transiting the Strait of Hormuz, prompting President Trump to declare the fragile ceasefire 'over' at the NATO summit in Turkey.
  • Oil pulls back after a wild Wednesday: WTI crude surged as high as ~$76/barrel during Wednesday's session before easing to around $74 Thursday morning (latest available market data, may be delayed). Wednesday's WTI settle of $73.52 marked a 4.4% single-day spike. The US also revoked a waiver that had previously allowed Iran to sell crude on global markets — a move that raises the medium-term supply stakes considerably.
  • AI trade stages a comeback: Despite the geopolitical backdrop, the Nasdaq is outperforming Thursday as semiconductor stocks bounce. A gauge of chip firms climbed roughly 4% off lows. Micron and Sandisk both posted premarket gains. The PHLX Semiconductor Index (SOX) is still down 16% from its June 22 peak and below its 50-day moving average, but today's session suggests dip buyers are returning. Alibaba surged 11% after a US judge ordered a reprieve from a Pentagon lobbying ban.
  • Fed minutes kept rates in focus: Minutes from the Fed's June FOMC meeting — released Wednesday — showed policymakers are split on the rate path, with roughly half expecting at least one additional hike before year-end. The 10-year Treasury yield hit a 4-week high near 4.58%, squeezing rate-sensitive sectors. One-year inflation expectations sit at 3.7%, and the next key catalyst is June CPI on July 14, followed by Congressional testimony from Fed Chair Kevin Warsh the same day. The big bank earnings parade (JPM, BAC, GS, WFC, C) also kicks off July 14.
  • PepsiCo beats, SK Hynix IPO demand scorching: PEP posted Q2 core EPS of $2.20 vs. the $2.19 consensus, with revenue rising 6.4% year-over-year to $24.18B, and backed its full-year outlook. South Korean memory giant SK Hynix's US listing is reportedly more than seven times oversubscribed, underscoring still-intense investor appetite for the AI chip supply chain ahead of its trading debut.
What to Watch

→ Geopolitics, hour by hour: With the US and Iran actively exchanging strikes and Strait of Hormuz shipping lanes under threat, any headline on escalation or de-escalation could move oil — and by extension the broader market — sharply. Iran's funeral procession for former Supreme Leader Khamenei adds another dimension of political uncertainty inside Tehran today.

→ Weekly jobless claims & June existing home sales: Both data points drop today and will give markets a fresh read on the labor market and the rate-sensitive housing sector. Existing home sales already came in at a seasonally adjusted 4.09M units — a 2.4% monthly decline, missing expectations of a slight gain. Jobless claims will be parsed closely given elevated rate fears.

→ SK Hynix US debut & CPI next week: SK Hynix prices its US offering Thursday with demand running at 7x oversubscribed — a real-time barometer for how hot the AI chip trade really is. Then eyes shift to July 14: June CPI + Fed Chair Warsh testimony + the start of major bank earnings season. That combo could reset the entire macro narrative for the summer.
